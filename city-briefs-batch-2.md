### Draft opening

Rome is a three-day city where the question of "where to stay" matters more than in most European capitals.

The center is walkable, but Rome is bigger than it looks on a map, and picking the wrong neighborhood means either taking the metro (which goes to three useful places) or taxiing (which costs more than it should and moves slower than walking).

If it's your first trip: stay in the **Centro Storico** near the Pantheon, Piazza Navona, or Campo de' Fiori. You'll walk to almost everything worth walking to, eat badly at the first restaurant you sit down at on a piazza, eat well at the second restaurant you find two streets off the piazza, and not need the metro once.

If you're prioritizing evening atmosphere over monument proximity: **Trastevere**. Stay there if you want to be in the neighborhood everyone else walks across the river to eat dinner in. Don't stay there if you're a light sleeper — it's loud until 2am on weekends.

If it's your second trip and you want something more local: **Monti** or **Testaccio**. Different flavors of the same answer — Monti is the trendy-local version, Testaccio is the food-serious version.

Skip the Termini area unless you have an early train. Skip the area near the Vatican if you want dinner options after 10pm. Skip Roma Nord entirely.

Here's how to pick.

### Draft opening

Barcelona has a where-to-stay problem nobody wants to talk about.

The apartment stock that made Barcelona cheap and flexible a decade ago is being phased out — the city is revoking tourist-apartment licenses through 2028, and by the time you're reading this, there are materially fewer Airbnbs than there were last year. Hotels are getting more crowded, more expensive, and less interesting to pick between.

The practical effect: picking the right neighborhood matters more than it used to, because you have less flexibility to rebook if you got it wrong.

Here's the short version.

**First-timers:** stay in **Eixample** — the grid district between Plaça Catalunya and Sagrada Família. You get central, walkable-to-Gaudí, good restaurant density, and hotel rooms bigger than a shoebox. It's the boring default answer and it's the right default answer.

**Couples and second-timers:** stay in **El Born** — quieter than the Gothic Quarter, a five-minute walk to both the Picasso Museum and the beach, and Barcelona's most walkable old neighborhood.

**Atmosphere-first travelers:** the **Gothic Quarter** works, but read the honest section below before booking. It's loud, pickpocket-heavy, and the medieval charm turns claustrophobic after 48 hours.

**Everyone avoiding tourists:** stay in **Gràcia** — feels like a separate small town bolted onto Barcelona, 20 minutes walk or one metro stop from Gaudí's Passeig de Gràcia.

**If you want the beach:** you have a choice to make about Barceloneta that most travel sites won't be honest about. Covered below.

### Draft opening

Amsterdam is small enough to walk across in an hour and dense enough that the neighborhood you sleep in changes what your days feel like.

If it's your first trip and you have three nights, stay in the **Canal Ring (Grachtengordel)** or **Jordaan**. Canal Ring is the postcard-central answer and the most expensive. Jordaan is the five-minute-walk-west answer — same canals, a quarter fewer tourists per square meter, more local cafes, fewer overpriced pancake restaurants.

If it's your second trip and you want restaurants Dutch people actually eat at: **De Pijp**. The Albert Cuyp Market is there, the best Turkish and Surinamese food is there, and your hotel will cost 30% less than the Canal Ring equivalent.

If you have four+ nights or are travelling with family, consider **Oud-West** — quieter, close to Vondelpark and the museums, and your kids can actually run around without threading between tour groups.

If you want to see the Amsterdam that's being built instead of preserved, stay in **Noord** across the IJ. Free ferries every few minutes, a proper skyline, and hotels at half the price for twice the room. Flag: you'll ferry back and forth three times a day.

Skip the streets inside De Wallen (Red Light District) if you don't want your hotel to be in a 2am party zone. More on that below — neither moral panic nor whitewash, just what it's actually like.

Amsterdam also has the highest hotel-tax rate in Europe (12.5% of your nightly room rate, as of 2024). Budget for it. Nobody warns you.

London has 32 boroughs and arguing about which one is "best to stay in" is a waste of your time. There are six neighborhoods worth considering and about four you should rule out without further thought.

The real question in London is not "which neighborhood" but "how close to a useful Tube station, on a line that goes where you actually want to go." If your hotel is a ten-minute walk from a Zone 1 tube station, and that station is on a line that touches two of the places you're spending time, you've won — regardless of whether the postcode starts with W1, WC2, SE1, or N1.

Here's the short version.

**First-timers:** stay in **Covent Garden** or **Soho** (WC2, W1). You'll walk to theater, eat better than you expected, spend more than you expected, and not take the Tube unless you want to. If the rates are eye-watering — and they will be — cross the river to **South Bank**. Same proximity to central attractions, a Thames view, and hotel rates 20–30% lower.

**Second-timers / design-hotel lovers:** **Shoreditch** or **Spitalfields** (E1, E2). East London is where the restaurants you'll remember are. You'll Tube in for central sights and not regret it.

**Couples:** consider **Marylebone** (central but calm) or **Notting Hill** (pretty, pricey, photogenic). Marylebone is the better value; Notting Hill is the better Instagram.

**Budget or family travelers who refuse to pay central London rates:** stay in **King's Cross / Bloomsbury** (WC1, N1). The area that had a reputation in the 2000s is now one of the best tube-hubs in central London (6 lines + Eurostar), has decent mid-range hotels, and costs 30–40% less per night than Covent Garden.

Skip Leicester Square hotels. Skip the Edgware Road / Paddington hotel strip unless you're catching the Heathrow Express. Skip anywhere in Zone 3+ unless you like long Tube rides.

Here's how to pick.

Berlin doesn't have neighborhoods. It has *Kieze* — the German word for a small area that functions like a village inside the city, where you have your own bakery, your own bar, your own Späti (corner shop open until midnight), and you don't really need to leave for a whole weekend.

Picking where to stay in Berlin is picking which Kiez you're going to pretend you live in for three days.

Here's the short version.

**First-timers on a short trip:** stay in **Mitte** near Alexanderplatz or Hackescher Markt. You're walking distance from the Brandenburg Gate, Museum Island, and most of the history-tour list. Pay for convenience; eat somewhere else.

**Second-timers / anyone under 40 wanting the Berlin travelers describe:** stay in **Kreuzberg** (specifically the area near Bergmannstraße or Schlesisches Tor). This is where you'll eat the best döner of your life, drink at a corner bar for €4, and walk past graffiti that's better than most gallery art.

**Couples who want trees and quiet evenings:** **Prenzlauer Berg**. Post-Wall's most gentrified neighborhood. Cafes with oat milk, cobblestoned streets, young families with cargo bikes, zero clubs. If that sounds boring, it's not for you. If it sounds perfect, book immediately.

**Clubbers heading for Berghain / RAW / About Blank:** **Friedrichshain**. Walk home at 6am instead of waiting for the U-Bahn.

**Travelers who want "proper German" — KaDeWe, Kurfürstendamm, Opera:** **Charlottenburg**. West Berlin's old money heart. Older crowd, better-preserved buildings, quieter streets.

**Long-stays, nomads, anyone watching budget carefully:** **Neukölln**. Gentrifying fast but still 30% cheaper than the same quality in Kreuzberg. Flag: Neukölln is huge; specific sub-areas vary widely. Covered below.

East vs West still matters in Berlin. Not politically anymore (pretty much nowhere, really), but architecturally and atmospherically — East feels different from West, and if you came to Berlin expecting East (graffiti, Plattenbau, raw) and book West (bourgeois, preserved, calm), you'll feel you booked the wrong city.

Here's how to pick.

### Draft opening

Cappadocia isn't a city — it's a region of small towns scattered across a volcanic landscape, and picking "where to stay" really means picking which small town to use as a base.

There are three that matter: **Göreme** (central, most hotels, balloons take off right above you), **Ürgüp** (15 minutes away, quieter, more upscale), and **Uçhisar** (10 minutes away, on top of a hill, dramatic views and fewer restaurants). Everything else is either day-trip distance (you'll drive in) or a specific niche.

Here's the short version.

If it's your first visit and your photo plan includes balloons over your room at sunrise: stay in **Göreme**. Accept that you'll wake up at 4:30am whether you wanted to or not, because the town is awake from 4 and the balloons fly from 5. The trade-off is the best photos, the closest walks to the open-air museum and hiking trails, and a dense restaurant scene.

If you want the balloon photos but with quieter mornings and a more polished hotel experience: **Ürgüp**. You'll drive or taxi to the balloon launch point but wake up in a calmer town. Ürgüp has Cappadocia's highest concentration of luxury cave hotels.

If you want isolation and photos over the valleys more than balloon-room proximity: **Uçhisar**. Fewer dining options, best views of anywhere in the region, and you'll watch balloons from above rather than be underneath them.

One thing nobody will tell you before you book: the "balloon-view" room premium is real, specific, and often exaggerated. Some hotels charge 2–3× for rooms that look in the same general direction the balloons fly — not necessarily rooms where you'll see balloons at eye level. Covered honestly below.
